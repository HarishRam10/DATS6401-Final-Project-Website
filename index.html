Feature Importance

Relationship to TenYearCHD

  • Systolic and Diastolic Blood Pressure

    Understanding healthy and unhealthy levels of systolic and diastolic blood pressure can allow for conducive comprehension of how hypertension can be a major risk factor for the risk of Coronary Heart Disease.

  • Systolic Blood Pressure, Hypertension and Medication

    Systolic Blood Pressure refers to how much pressure blood is exerting against the artery walls when the heart beats. https://www.heart.org/en/health-topics/high-blood-pressure/understanding-blood-pressure-readings

  • Diastolic Blood Pressure, Hypertension and Medication

    Diastolic Blood Pressure indicates how much pressure blood exerts against the artery walls while the heart is resting between beats.

  • BMI and Total Cholesterol

    A high BMI or Total Cholesterol can lead to a build up of cholesterol in the arteries which can block blood going to the heart.

  • Feature Importance

    Using Machine Learning, we can detect certain features that are most influential in predicting the target variable, TenYearCHD.

The correlation matrix above shows the strength of relationships between features in the dataset. The darker the shade of blue, the stronger the correlation. Systolic and Diastolic Blood Pressure are highly correlated to each other and Prevalent Hypertension - similar to Diabetes and Glucose. Although difficult to tell, TenYearCHD seems to have a non-zero correlation with Prevalent Hypertension, Blood Pressure and Age.

This column chart demonstrates the distribution of patients that are postive for TenYear CHD by Age Group and Gender. The number of patients increase significantly as age increases, but varies between Genders. Men, ages 50 to 54, and Women, ages 55 to 59, seem to have the highest density of TenYearCHD.

Although education level cannot be considered a direct risk factor, it is interesting to see the distribution of TenYearCHD positive patients decrease as education level increases. Therefore, the largest group of people in this study that tested positive for TenYearCHD within 10 years also had the lowest level of education.

The scatter plot on the left shows the relationship between Glucose and Total Cholesterol where yellow dots indicate instances in which the patient was diabetic and purple indicating non-diabetic. Most of the instances of diabetes occur at high glucose levels indicating a strong correlation between the variables. The column chart on the right displays average levels of Glucose by Gender for those with and without Diabetes. This graphic reinforces the idea that Glucose and Diabetes have a strong correlation.

The swarmchart above shows the distribution of patients with Diabetes in relation to the risk of TenYearCHD. Although the sample sizes are significantly different, there is a larger cluster of TenYearCHD positive cases in patients above 50 as opposed to below regardless of the presence of diabetes.

The scatter plot shows the relationship between Glucose and Age with yellow dots indicating patients who have actually developed Coronary Heart Disease within 10 years. The areas of the chart where the yellow dots are clustered are focused on older people and those with higher Glucose levels. This graph reinforces the idea that age and high levels of glucose contribute to a higher likelihood of contracting Coranary Heart Disease.

The scatter plots above show the relationship between Systolic and Diastolic Blood Pressure. The first graph on the left-most side highlights the instances of hypertension in yellow. Anything to the left of the vertical line in the center of the chart demonstrates instances of healthy levels of Diastolic Blood Pressure while anything below the horizontal line indicates patients with healthy levels of Systolic Blood Pressure.

In the second and third graph, the horizontal lines indicate the average Diastolic Blood Pressure while the vertical lines show the average Systolic Blood Pressure. The middle graph shows a significant increase in the average Systolic Blood Pressure for CHD Positive patients. The last graph for CHD Negative patients shows values much closer to the healthy level

These three graphs show average levels of Systolic Blood Pressures across different Age Groups with the introduction of hypertensive patients in the second graphs and patients who take blood pressure medication in the third graph. All patients visualized in the first graph were CHD Positive without any pre-existing conditions or known medication. The systolic blood pressure for each age group is either close to or past the healthy level of 120 mm Hg.

In the second graph, patients with Hypertension were added to the sample and the average Systolic Blood Pressure rose significantly for every age group. The average Systolic Blood Pressure regardless of age or sex is above 120 mm Hg.

Finally, with the introduction of Blood Pressure Medication, all the averages decrease considerably. The purpose of these graphs is to show how Hypertension influences Systolic Blood Pressure which already has a positive correlation with TenYearCHD.

The purpose of this double box and whisker plot is to show how the average heart rate for those with and without TenYearCHD is very similar. The only difference between each column is the minimum and maximum values, so there is not enough information to safely demonstrate a higher average resting heart rate for those with TenYearCHD. However, there is a clear overall increase in the interquartile range for those who do smoke compared to non-smokers.

The bar chart above is separated into two sections - the left being those who are negative for TenYearCHD and positive on the right. The entire sample consists of only smokers. There is a clear discrepancy in the number of cigarettes per day for TenYearCHD Positive and Negative patients. It cannot be said that those who smoke a lot of cigarettes ultimately contract Coronary Heart Disease; however, smoking puts much more strain on the heart and forces it took work much harder to perform the same function by causing a build-up of plaque in the arteries.

These 3 graphs show average Diastolic Blood Pressure across several age groups consisting of only patients who were positive for TenYear CHD. These graphs resonate with the previous graphs representing systolic blood pressure, but show different results.

The first graph shows each age group having an average diastolic blood pressure near or above the healthy level of 80 mm Hg. Focusing on the y-axis scale, the second graph which includes hypertensive patients shows a clear average increase in each age group.

The last graph introduces Blood Pressure medication, but does not have the same consderable decrease as the affect of blood pressure medication on systolic blood pressure.

These graphs demonstrate how a higher Total Cholsterol and BMI is associated to TenYearCHD. In the double box and whisker plot, we can see that for those with CHD regardless of sex, the entire interquartile range of Total Cholesterol increases as well as the min and max. On the scatter plot to the left, the blue dots indicate patients that are positive for TenYearCHD and dotted line indicates a healthy BMI of 25 for adults. A majority of the blue dots can be seen clustered above the healthy cut-off indicating that those with a higher BMI are more likely to have a 10 year risk of coronary heart disease.

The bar chart above was created by applying the Random Forest Classification (Decision Tree) algorithm on this dataset. As a result of the class imbalance of the target variable, a balanced class weight was added add extra weight the minority sample which, in this case, was positive TenYearCHD instances.

The algorithm detected Age, Systolic Blood Pressure, Total Cholesterol, Diastolic Blood Pressure and BMI as the most influential variables in predicting the outcome TenYearCHD. This graph reinforce all the relationships and meaningful conclusions extracted from analysis of previous visualizations using machine learning.
